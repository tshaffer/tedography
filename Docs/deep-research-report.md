# Fasting Plasma Glucose vs Hemoglobin A1c for Predicting Future Type 2 Diabetes

## Executive summary

Across major cohort studies and systematic reviews, a single **fasting plasma glucose (FPG)** and a single **hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)** are **both strong predictors** of future type 2 diabetes (T2D), with **no universal ÔÇťwinnerÔÇŁ** across all settings. Relative advantages depend on (a) *what outcome definition is used* (clinically diagnosed diabetes vs screen-detected diabetes that includes FPG or A1c in the definition), (b) *time horizon*, and (c) *population factors* (baseline insulin sensitivity, age, and conditions/medications affecting either test). ţłÇciteţłéturn27view0ţłéturn22view0ţłéturn19view0ţłéturn29view0ţłü

In the ţłÇentityţłé["organization","Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities (ARIC) Study","us cohort study"]ţłü analysis published in ţłÇentityţłé["organization","The New England Journal of Medicine","medical journal"]ţłü, models using HbA1c achieved **similar discrimination** for future diabetes compared with models using fasting glucose; adding HbA1c to models containing fasting glucose improved risk classification for some outcomes, and HbA1c remained associated with incident diabetes in models adjusting for fasting glucose. ţłÇciteţłéturn27view0ţłéturn28view0ţłü

Across pooled prognosis evidence from a ţłÇentityţłé["organization","Cochrane Collaboration","systematic review org"]ţłü review of ÔÇťintermediate hyperglycaemia,ÔÇŁ hazard ratios for incident T2D are **of similar order** for common ÔÇťprediabetesÔÇŁ definitions: **IFG (FPG 5.6ÔÇô6.9 mmol/L; 100ÔÇô125 mg/dL)** and **HbA1c 5.7ÔÇô6.4%** both confer multi-fold increased risk versus normoglycaemia; higher cut-points (e.g., WHO IFG 6.1ÔÇô6.9 mmol/L or HbA1c Ôëą6.0%) generally mark substantially higher risk, albeit with high heterogeneity. ţłÇciteţłéturn25view0ţłéturn23view0ţłéturn24view0ţłü

Where head-to-head comparisons are available in the same dataset, **combining FPG and HbA1c consistently identifies a higher-risk subgroup than either alone** (often with marked improvements in risk discrimination or reclassification). ţłÇciteţłéturn33view0ţłéturn19view0ţłéturn24view0ţłéturn31view0ţłü

Clinical implication: for **screening**, HbA1c is often operationally preferable (nonfasting, lower within-person variability), but FPG is inexpensive and widely available. For **risk stratification**, either marker is informative; if one is borderline or discordant with the other, **repeat/confirm and consider combined testing and/or OGTT** depending on pretest probability and whether assay interference is plausible. ţłÇciteţłéturn32view0ţłéturn29view0ţłü

## Definitions and physiologic basis

### What each test measures and the time window reflected

**Fasting plasma glucose (FPG)** is an *acute* measure of circulating glucose after an overnight fast (typically Ôëą8 hours). It reflects the net balance of hepatic glucose production and peripheral glucose disposal in the fasting state, and exhibits relatively high within-person variability and susceptibility to acute perturbations (recent illness, stress, physical activity, sample processing delays). ţłÇciteţłéturn29view0ţłéturn32view0ţłü

**HbA1c** is a *chronic* measure of glycaemic exposure. It reflects glucose bound to hemoglobin over the erythrocyte lifespan (~120 days), making it a weighted average that is more influenced by recent weeks but still integrates ~2ÔÇô3 months of exposure. HbA1c is less affected by acute day-to-day factors (food intake, short-term stress/illness/activity) and has lower within-person variability, but is biased by altered red-cell turnover and certain hemoglobin variants or clinical states (e.g., anemia/iron status, transfusion, hemolysis, kidney failure/dialysis, pregnancy, cirrhosis, HIV, etc.). ţłÇciteţłéturn32view0ţłéturn29view0ţłü

### Guideline thresholds commonly used

The ţłÇentityţłé["organization","American Diabetes Association","diabetes guideline body"]ţłü Standards of Care define, in nonpregnant adults:  
- **Prediabetes**: HbA1c 5.7ÔÇô6.4% or FPG 100ÔÇô125 mg/dL (IFG) (or OGTT 2-h 140ÔÇô199 mg/dL). ţłÇciteţłéturn29view0ţłü  
- **Diabetes**: HbA1c Ôëą6.5% or FPG Ôëą126 mg/dL (or OGTT 2-h Ôëą200 mg/dL, or random glucose Ôëą200 mg/dL with classic symptoms). ţłÇciteţłéturn29view0ţłü  
They also recommend confirmatory testing (repeat same test or a different test) absent unequivocal hyperglycaemia and provide explicit instructions for discordant results (repeat the test above the diagnostic threshold and evaluate potential assay interference). ţłÇciteţłéturn29view0ţłéturn32view0ţłü

The ţłÇentityţłé["organization","World Health Organization","un agency health"]ţłü consultation report recommends HbA1c **Ôëą6.5%** as a diagnostic cut-point for diabetes (with important caveats about assay standardization and conditions affecting validity). ţłÇciteţłéturn30search0ţłéturn30search8ţłü

## Comparative predictive performance for incident type 2 diabetes

This section prioritizes evidence that (a) reports prospective prediction of incident diabetes and (b) enables direct or near-direct comparison between FPG and HbA1c, while noting common methodological pitfalls (especially incorporation bias when the outcome definition includes the biomarker being evaluated). ţłÇciteţłéturn29view0ţłéturn24view0ţłéturn9view0ţłü

### Cohort evidence directly comparing HbA1c and fasting glucose

In the ARIC cohort analysis (Selvin et al., 2010), HbA1c categories showed a steep graded association with incident diagnosed diabetes over long follow-up; for example, compared with HbA1c 5.0ÔÇô<5.5%, hazard ratios for diagnosed diabetes rose progressively across HbA1c strata (including very high risk at HbA1c Ôëą6.5%). ţłÇciteţłéturn27view0ţłéturn28view0ţłü

The same paper presents parallel modeling for fasting glucose categories, showing that **FPG 100ÔÇô<126 mg/dL** (vs <100) is associated with materially higher incident diabetes risk, and that discrimination (HarrellÔÇÖs C) for diabetes prediction using HbA1c and using fasting glucose is broadly similar after standard covariate adjustment. ţłÇciteţłéturn27view0ţłéturn28view0ţłéturn28view1ţłü

A key clinician-oriented interpretation of this ARIC evidence: when used as *baseline* predictors, **HbA1c and fasting glucose both predict incident diabetes strongly; neither dominates across all models**, and combined modeling improves overall discrimination for endpoints where both carry independent signal. ţłÇciteţłéturn27view0ţłéturn28view0ţłéturn28view1ţłü

### Pooled prognosis evidence for ÔÇťprediabetes-rangeÔÇŁ glycaemia

The Cochrane prognosis review (Richter et al., 2018) synthesizes hazard ratios for incident T2D across definitions of intermediate hyperglycaemia. In pooled analyses:  
- **IFG 5.6 (FPG 5.6ÔÇô6.9 mmol/L)** vs normoglycaemia: HR Ôëł **4.32** (with very high heterogeneity across regions/studies). ţłÇciteţłéturn25view0ţłéturn24view0ţłü  
- **IFG 6.1 (FPG 6.1ÔÇô6.9 mmol/L)** vs normoglycaemia: HR Ôëł **5.47**. ţłÇciteţłéturn25view0ţłéturn24view0ţłü  
- **HbA1c 5.7ÔÇô6.4%** vs normoglycaemia: HR Ôëł **5.55** (substantial between-study heterogeneity; markedly different pooled estimates by region). ţłÇciteţłéturn23view0ţłü  
- **HbA1c 6.0ÔÇô6.4%** vs normoglycaemia: HR Ôëł **10.10** (very high heterogeneity). ţłÇciteţłéturn23view0ţłü  

While hazard ratios are not ÔÇťAUC equivalents,ÔÇŁ their magnitudes support a pragmatic conclusion: **at common guideline cut-points, HbA1c-based and FPG-based intermediate hyperglycaemia identify comparably elevated risk**, with higher thresholds (6.1 mmol/L IFG or HbA1c Ôëą6.0%) selecting a smaller, higher-risk group. ţłÇciteţłéturn23view0ţłéturn25view0ţłéturn29view0ţłü

### Evidence on discrimination and reclassification when adding glycaemic measures

A very large Chinese prospective cohort analysis (Diabetes Care 2019) evaluated how adding glycaemic measures improves prediction of incident diabetes beyond conventional risk factors. Adding **FPG** increased the C-statistic by **╬öC Ôëł 0.043**, while adding **HbA1c** increased it by **╬öC Ôëł 0.034**; adding both together improved discrimination more than either alone (╬öC Ôëł 0.058), and adding all three (FPG, 2h-PG, HbA1c) improved further (╬öC Ôëł 0.088). ţłÇciteţłéturn19view0ţłéturn18view0ţłü  

The same study reports multivariable risk ratios for incident diabetes over a mean ~3.8-year follow-up: prediabetes defined by **FPG 100ÔÇô125 mg/dL** (RR Ôëł **2.03**) and by **HbA1c 5.7ÔÇô6.4%** (RR Ôëł **1.85**) are both predictive; when both are included simultaneously with 2h-PG, the independent associations of IFG-range FPG and elevated HbA1c become similar in magnitude (RR Ôëł **1.46** and **1.44**, respectively), while 2h-PG retains stronger independent predictive value. ţłÇciteţłéturn18view0ţłéturn19view0ţłü

### Absolute risk estimates over common time horizons

Absolute risk is highly context- and population-dependent (baseline glycaemia distribution, age, BMI, competing risks, ascertainment method). The strongest clinically useful pattern is that **risk escalates steeply toward the upper end of ÔÇťprediabetesÔÇŁ ranges**, especially when multiple tests are abnormal. ţłÇciteţłéturn23view0ţłéturn33view0ţłéturn8search16ţłéturn19view0ţłü

Representative estimates include:

- **~4-year horizon (mean 3.8 years)** in a very large Chinese cohort: isolated IFG had ~**5.0%** cumulative incidence, isolated elevated HbA1c ~**4.7%**, combined IFG+elevated HbA1c ~**9.2%**, and triple abnormality (IFG + IGT + elevated HbA1c) ~**21.3%**. ţłÇciteţłéturn18view0ţłü  
- **~5-year horizon** in the Exeter 10,000 cohort: the 5-year risk of developing diabetes (HbA1c-defined) rises within the ÔÇťprediabetesÔÇŁ bandÔÇöe.g., ~**2.1%** for HbA1c 5.7ÔÇô5.9%, ~**7.0%** for 6.0ÔÇô6.1%, and ~**26.4%** for 6.2ÔÇô6.4%. ţłÇciteţłéturn8search16ţłü  
- **~10-year horizon** in ARIC (risk-stratification paper): among those already at very high glycaemia, having both FPG Ôëą126 mg/dL and HbA1c Ôëą6.5% at baseline corresponded to an estimated **10-year risk ~88%**, versus ~55% with FPG Ôëą126 but HbA1c 5.7ÔÇô<6.5%, illustrating the value of combined measures at the high end. ţłÇciteţłéturn31view0ţłéturn6view0ţłü  

## How predictive performance varies by population and follow-up interval

### Age and competing risks

In older adults, competing risks (mortality) and regression to normoglycaemia materially change the apparent predictive utility of intermediate hyperglycaemia. In the ţłÇentityţłé["organization","JAMA Internal Medicine","medical journal"]ţłü ARIC analysis of older adults (mean age ~76), over ~5 years, only ~**8ÔÇô9%** of those meeting HbA1c- or fasting glucoseÔÇôbased ÔÇťprediabetesÔÇŁ progressed to diabetes, while regression or death was more frequent than progression. ţłÇciteţłéturn20view0ţłéturn21search0ţłü

This does not mean FPG or HbA1c are ÔÇťbad testsÔÇŁ in older adults; it means **absolute risk is lower than clinicians often assume** when ÔÇťprediabetesÔÇŁ is defined solely by a single lab value, and counseling should incorporate competing risk and patient goals. ţłÇciteţłéturn20view0ţłéturn21search0ţłü

### BMI, insulin resistance, and baseline glycaemia distribution

Observed predictive performance shifts with baseline insulin resistance and glycaemia distribution. For example, the Hoorn Study found FPG to have the strongest association with incident diabetes, but it explicitly notes that this may reflect the fact that incident diabetes was frequently diagnosed based on FPG, inflating the apparent strength of FPG relative to HbA1c (incorporation/ascertainment bias). ţłÇciteţłéturn9view0ţłü

Guidelines emphasize that predictive associations are broadly continuous (risk rises across the distribution), and that ÔÇťprediabetesÔÇŁ cut-points are pragmatic rather than mechanistic. ţłÇciteţłéturn29view0ţłéturn32view0ţłü

### Race/ethnicity and conditions that bias HbA1c

HbA1c can differ by race/ethnicity and by hematologic factors independent of glucose; in ARIC, race modified some associations for longer-term self-reported diabetes, and contemporary guidance stresses evaluating discordance and potential interferences rather than using race as a proxy for biology. ţłÇciteţłéturn27view0ţłéturn29view0ţłéturn32view0ţłü

Clinically, HbA1c may be less reliable in settings with altered erythrocyte turnover or hemoglobin variants; in such cases, FPG (or OGTT) may be preferable for risk assessment and diagnosis. ţłÇciteţłéturn32view0ţłéturn30search0ţłéturn30search8ţłü

### Pregnancy and postpartum contexts

For gestational diabetes screening/diagnosis, major guidelines prioritize OGTT-based strategies rather than HbA1c or FPG alone; HbA1cÔÇÖs physiology and performance in pregnancy differ, and pregnancy itself is listed among contexts affecting HbA1c interpretation. ţłÇciteţłéturn29view0ţłéturn32view0ţłü

### Follow-up interval and decay of predictive accuracy

Prediction from a single baseline biomarker generally weakens over longer horizons because metabolic status changes. In ARIC-derived analyses evaluating HbA1c for *incident* diabetes, near-term AUC (Ôëł6 years) was higher than longer-horizon discrimination (Ôëł14 years), illustrating time-dependent decay in predictive ability from a single baseline value. ţłÇciteţłéturn6view0ţłéturn31view1ţłü

## Combined use and multimarker prediction

### FPG plus HbA1c versus either alone

Multiple datasets show that combining FPG and HbA1c is clinically useful for identifying a high-risk subgroup:

- In Health ABC (older adults), odds of incident diabetes over 7 years were far higher when both IFG and elevated HbA1c were present (OR ~26) than when either abnormality was present alone; adding HbA1c to IFG improved discrimination and calibration. ţłÇciteţłéturn33view0ţłü  
- In a large Chinese cohort, adding both FPG and HbA1c improved discrimination more than either alone (╬öC 0.058 vs ╬öC 0.043 or 0.034) and reclassification (NRI increments reported), supporting combined use for risk stratification. ţłÇciteţłéturn19view0ţłéturn18view0ţłü  
- In pooled evidence, combined IFG + HbA1c definitions yield very large relative risk compared with normoglycaemia (noting that effect sizes can be inflated by differences in ascertainment and baseline risk). ţłÇciteţłéturn24view0ţłéturn23view0ţłü  

### OGTT and other markers

While this report centers on FPG vs HbA1c, substantial evidence indicates that **post-load glucose (OGTT 2h-PG)** can add independent predictive information beyond FPG and HbA1c in some populations and often improves discrimination more than either alone. This is emphasized both in large cohort analyses and in guideline-level statements that OGTT is ÔÇťmore sensitiveÔÇŁ for detecting dysglycaemia. ţłÇciteţłéturn19view0ţłéturn32view0ţłéturn29view0ţłü

Markers of insulin resistance (fasting insulin, HOMA-IR) can associate with diabetes risk, but in some cohort comparisons their predictive associations are weaker than FPG or HbA1c, and they are less standardized for routine clinical screening. ţłÇciteţłéturn9view0ţłü

## Practical clinical implications

### Which test to use for screening vs risk stratification

**Screening (asymptomatic, nonpregnant adults):** Both HbA1c and FPG are acceptable, but HbA1c is operationally attractive because it is nonfasting and less variable; FPG is cheaper and widely available. Many clinicians use HbA1c as a first-line screening test when no factors are present that would invalidate HbA1c. ţłÇciteţłéturn32view0ţłéturn29view0ţłü

**Risk stratification (borderline results, high-risk individuals):** Either test provides meaningful risk gradient, but combined testing is often more informative than either test alone, especially when results are near actionable thresholds, discordant, or when a prevention program referral is being considered. ţłÇciteţłéturn33view0ţłéturn19view0ţłéturn29view0ţłü

### When to repeat testing and when to order the other test

Per ADA guidance, if results are close to diagnostic thresholds, clinicians should educate patients about symptoms and repeat testing within **3ÔÇô6 months**; for discordant results between two tests, repeat the test that is above the diagnostic cut-point and evaluate potential interferences. ţłÇciteţłéturn29view0ţłéturn32view0ţłü

From a prediction standpoint (not just diagnosis): repeating a borderline abnormality reduces misclassification due to FPG variability and improves the clinical signal-to-noise of a ÔÇťprediabetesÔÇŁ label. ţłÇciteţłéturn6view0ţłéturn32view0ţłü

### Counseling patients with discordant results

A common scenario is **FPG ~100ÔÇô125 mg/dL with HbA1c <5.7%**, or the reverse. Practical counseling points supported by the evidence base are:

- Discordance is expected because the tests capture different physiology and time windows (acute fasting hepatic output vs integrated glycaemic exposure) and because each has distinct sources of measurement error. ţłÇciteţłéturn32view0ţłéturn27view0ţłü  
- Risk generally increases with higher values of either marker, but the **highest near- to mid-term risk** occurs when *both* are abnormal. ţłÇciteţłéturn33view0ţłéturn19view0ţłéturn18view0ţłü  
- If HbA1c appears inconsistent with glucose values, evaluate for HbA1c interferences (anemia, hemoglobin variants, transfusion, kidney failure/dialysis, pregnancy) and consider plasma glucoseÔÇôbased testing (FPG and/or OGTT) or alternative chronic glycaemia markers where appropriate. ţłÇciteţłéturn32view0ţłéturn29view0ţłéturn30search0ţłü

## Limitations, biases, and gaps in evidence

Comparative studies of ÔÇťwhich is betterÔÇŁ are frequently affected by **incorporation bias**: if incident diabetes is defined using FPG (or HbA1c), the corresponding baseline biomarker can appear artificially stronger because it is closer to the ascertainment mechanism. The Hoorn Study explicitly notes this issue, and similar concerns apply broadly across cohorts. ţłÇciteţłéturn9view0ţłéturn24view0ţłü

**Generalizability** is limited by heterogeneity in baseline risk, healthcare access, and follow-up intensity. Cochrane pooled estimates show high heterogeneity across regions and study designs, indicating that hazard ratios at the same laboratory cut-points can differ materially across populations. ţłÇciteţłéturn23view0ţłéturn25view0ţłéturn24view0ţłü

**Time horizons matter:** predictive performance from a single baseline test degrades over longer follow-up, and many studies report only one time scale. ţłÇciteţłéturn31view1ţłéturn6view0ţłéturn27view0ţłü

**HbA1c interferences** are clinically critical but inconsistently handled across epidemiologic datasets; many cohorts exclude some conditions but cannot fully capture shifts in erythrocyte turnover over time. ţłÇciteţłéturn32view0ţłéturn30search0ţłéturn30search8ţłü

Evidence on **net reclassification improvement (NRI)** and other incremental metrics is informative but can be sensitive to modeling assumptions and chosen risk categories; clinicians should treat modest improvements in ╬öC-statistic as potentially meaningful at the population level but not always decisive for individual decisions. ţłÇciteţłéturn19view0ţłéturn27view0ţłü
